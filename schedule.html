Module 1: Internet and the Web - Introduction and Key Elements
Lecture 1: Introduction to CS425 Notes
Learning Objectives:
  • Become familiar with the CS425 syllabus, structure, contract and expectations.
  • Understand what to do and what to avoid while attending the class and studying.
  • Understand the implications of the Internet on cognitive capabilities and social practices of humans. How are human learning and focus affected in the Internet era.
  • Understand and explain what is the Internet and how it has evolved.
  • Understand and explain the concept of network effects and "Metcalf's Law."
Readings and Videos:
  • CS425 contract.
  • What is the Internet? A 3:5min video with Vint Cerf
  • A 30:22 min video on the history of Arpanet, Internet's precursor: Computer Networks - The Heralds Of Resource Sharing (Arpanet, 1972)
  • "Have Smartphones Destroyed a Generation?" The Atlantic, September 2017, Jean Twenge.
  • "The Benefits of No-Tech Note Taking." The Chronicle of Higher Education, 4/3/2015, Carol Holstead.
  • Metcalf's Law
Lecture 2: Internet Protocols Learning Objectives:
  • Understand, explain, and analyze the concepts of protocols and protocol layering.
  • Understand, describe and explain the key protocols of the Internet and their functionality.
  • Understand, describe and explain the concepts of best-effort and hourglass architecture.
  • Understand, describe and explain the role and basic functionality of TCP/IP protocols.
Readings and Videos:
  • Chapter 1. "Web Programming", Step, Miller & Kirst.
  • The Internet: Packets, Routing & Reliability A 6:25min video.
Lecture 3: End-to-end principles and Net Neutrality Learning Objectives:
  • Understand and explain what end-to-end principles are about and how they have affected the design of the Internet.
  • Understand and explain the concept of Net Neutrality and its implications in the evolution of Internet services.
Readings:
  • Chapter 1. "Web Programming", Step, Miller & Kirst.
  • Internet Society's Policy Brief on Net Neutrality.
  • The European Commission on Net Neutrality.
  • Open Internet and Net Neutrality in Europe European Commission (2011).
  • "Why Net Neutrality matters even in the age of oligopoly", Klint Finley. WIRED, 22/6/2017.
Videos:
  • Net Neutrality: What Does the Future Hold? Video of a 2018 workshop at the Center for Information Technology Policy, Princeton University.
  • Professor Nicholas Economides of NYU Stern School of Business testimony before Congress on Net Neutrality (5min video and text).
Lecture 4: IP Addresses, DNS and Autonomous Systems
Learning Objectives:
  • Understand and explain the meaning of IP addresses.
  • Understand and explain the concept of Naming Services and their functionality.
  • Describe what DNS is and how it works.
  • Understand and explain the concept of Autonomous Systems.
Readings:
  • Domain Name System on Wikipedia.
  • Naming and Addressing, an overview of W3C materials related to Addressing.
Lecture 5: From Hypertext to the World-Wide Web
Learning Objectives:
  • Get acquainted with the Definition, History, Evolution, Impact and Key Ideas behind the World-Wide Web.
  • Understand and explain the main elements of the Web.
  • Understand and explain how the Web concept evolves, and how Web application development is different from traditional software development.
  • Understand,explain and apply the concepts of URI, URL and URN, information resource, name resolution, dereference, and retrieval.
  • Become familiar with different URI schemes.
  • Review what is Web Science and key concepts of Web analysis.
Readings:
  • "Multimedia and Hypertext: The Internet and Beyond." J. Nielsen. AP Professional, 1995.
  • Chapter 1. "Web Programming", Step, Miller & Kirst.
  • The Uniform Resource Identifier (URI): Generic Syntax (IETF).
  • Web Science: An Interdisciplinary Approach to Understanding the Web. James Hendler, Nigel Shadbolt, Wendy Hall, Tim Berners-Lee, and Daniel Weitzner. Communications of the ACM. Vol. 51, No. 7, July 2008. pp. 60-69.
Additional Readings:
  • "As we may think," by Vannevar Bush, The Atlantic (July 1945).
Videos:
  • "First thought, best thought.” J. Lanier talks about Ted Nelson at Chapman University, 2014.
Module 2: Web Infrastructure and Protocols
Lecture 6: Distributed Computing Models
Learning Objectives:
  • Understand and explain the Client-Server computing and other distributed computing models.
  • Understand, explain and identify the role and location of clients, servers, proxies, and load balancers.
  • Describe the Web system architecture and explain the components of three-tier Web-site architecture and their role.
  • Understand, explain and apply the concepts of latency, bandwidth and load balacing.
  • Understand and explain the concept of Web Intermediaries and their functionality.
  • Understand, explain and analyze Web caching and its impact on Web performance.
Readings:
  • "Designing distributed applications with mobile code paradigms" by A. Carzaniga, G. Pietro Picco and G. Vigna, Proceeding ICSE '97 Proceedings of the 19th international conference on Software engineering, 1997.
  • The Client-Server Model (Wikipedia).
Lecture 7: Cloud Computing: History, Evolution and Key Elements
Learning outcome:
  • Understand and describe the history of Cloud Computing emergence.
  • Understand the concept of locality and explain its meaning.
  • Understand and explain the definition of Cloud Computing.
  • Understand, explain and apply the main Cloud Computing delivery models.
  • Understand and explain the concepts of Utility Computing, Elasticity.
  • Understand, explain, and design the components of a Cluster and a Warehouse Scale Computer.
Readings:
  • M. Armbrust et al.,"A view of cloud computing." Communications of the ACM, vol. 53, no. 4, p. 50, 2010.
  • Chapters 1,2. Peterson, Baker, Bavier, Williams and Davie. "Edge Cloud Operations: Operations: A Systems Approach."
Video:
  • Computing in the Cloud by Ed Felten (2008).
Lecture 8: The HTTP Protocol: Basic Elements
Learning outcome:
  • Learn, describe and explain the key elements of HTTP.
  • Demonstrate basic familiarity with the HTTP Protocol, its functionality and syntax.
  • Understand and explain the key concepts of HTTP: statelessness, headers, caching support, pipelining.
Readings:
  • The HTTP Protocol.
  • "HTTP: The Definitive Guide." David Gourly and Brian Totty. O'Reilly, 2002.
  • Lucian Popa, Patrick Wendell, Ali Ghodsi, Ion Stoica. "HTTP: An Evolvable Narrow Waist for the Future Internet." Technical Report No. UCB/EECS-2012-5, UC Berkeley, 2012.
Lecture 9: The HTTP Protocol: Advanced Topics
Learning outcome:
  • Discuss the main changes brought by SPDY and HTTP/2.
  • Understand the concept of multiplexing and server push.
Readings:
  • The HTTP Protocol.
  • "HTTP: The Definitive Guide." David Gourly and Brian Totty. O'Reilly, 2002.
  • Lucian Popa, Patrick Wendell, Ali Ghodsi, Ion Stoica. "HTTP: An Evolvable Narrow Waist for the Future Internet." Technical Report No. UCB/EECS-2012-5, UC Berkeley, 2012.
Lecture 10: Representational State Transfer (REST)
Learning Objectives:
  • Understand, explain, and apply the REST principles in Web services design and implementation.
  • Understand, explain, and apply REST principles for naming Web resources.
  • Understand, describe, and apply the Microservices architectural paradigm for the design and implementation of Web services and applications.
Readings:
  • Representational state transfer (Wikipedia).
  • URL as UI.
  • Microservices (2014) M. Fowler and J. Lewis.
  • Microservices: Decomposing Applications for Deployability and Scalability (2014) C. Richardson, Infoq.
  • "HTTP: The Definitive Guide." David Gourly and Brian Totty. O'Reilly, 2002.
